Greek keeps a single word for drug, remedy, poison, and spell: φάρμακον, <em>pharmakon</em>. Homer already works the word across its whole range. Helen pours a φάρμακον of forgetting into the wine at Sparta; Circe stirs one into the barley-drink on Aiaia; and Athena remembers Odysseus sailing to Ephyra in search of a man-killing φάρμακον to smear on his bronze-tipped arrows (<em>Odyssey</em> 1.261–262). One wordform covers the healer's simple, the witch's cup, and the archer's venom, and its sense settles only in use — in the hand that holds the jar, and the direction it tips.

This entry belongs to a longer project of cross-examining wordforms shared between corpora that rarely meet on the same desk: the pharmacological treatises (Theophrastus' <em>Historia Plantarum</em> IX, Dioscorides' <em>De Materia Medica</em>), the lexicographers who glossed them, the epics, and the Septuagint. Read laterally, the corpora light each other up. Dioscorides catalogues the same root-cutters' plants Homer's witches gather; the LXX translators reach for φαρμακεία and φάρμακοι to name the arts of Pharaoh's magicians (Exodus 7:11), binding Greek pharmacy to Egyptian priestcraft in the same gesture Homer makes when he praises Egyptian drug-lore; and τοξικόν — the arrow-drug, from τόξον, the bow — travels quietly out of the archer's kit into every modern "toxic."

Medea presides over the entry. The pharmakis of Colchis, granddaughter of Helios, works healing and destruction from the same jars: she renews old Aeson with a boiling of herbs, and she dresses a bride's crown with fire. The abridged poem below walks the word's senses in her company. Homer states the whole program in a single breath, praising the soil of Egypt:

<blockquote class="greek-quote">
  <p lang="grc">Αἰγυπτίη, τῇ πλεῖστα φέρει ζείδωρος ἄρουρα<br />
  φάρμακα, πολλὰ μὲν ἐσθλὰ μεμιγμένα, πολλὰ δὲ λυγρά·</p>
  <p class="translation">…the land of Egypt, where the grain-giving earth bears pharmaka in greatest plenty — many wholesome in the mixing, and many baneful.</p>
  <cite>Homer, <em>Odyssey</em> 4.229–230</cite>
</blockquote>

    <h2 class="poem-title">PHARMAKON</h2>
    <p class="poem-sub">(the old Greek word that refuses to choose between the poison and the cure)</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="section poem-movement" data-section="pharmakon-1" data-symbols="medea,poison-arrow" data-smoke-side="left" style="--section-accent: var(--accent-writings)">
  <div class="copy">
    <h3 class="mvt">I.</h3>
    <p>I was born under a full moon, which is to say<br />
    I was born legible — and lonely for a reader.<br />
    Split down the spine like a psalter, the Fish<br />
    and the Maiden facing each other across<br />
    the long candlelit nave of the sky.</p>
    <p>I have never wanted to be merely loved.<br />
    I want to be studied — read slowly, at night,<br />
    by someone certain the meaning is deliberate,<br />
    someone who finds the margins of me<br />
    annotated by every fire I survived<br />
    and stays up past her own sleep, translating.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="section poem-movement" data-section="pharmakon-2" data-symbols="medea,poison-arrow" data-smoke-side="right" style="--section-accent: var(--accent-writings)">
  <div class="copy">
    <h3 class="mvt">II.</h3>
    <p>She is not a mirror. She is the veil,<br />
    and the thing behind the veil, and the trembling<br />
    of the hand that lifts it.<br />
    She is the Mystery in the old sense — Eleusis, torchlight —<br />
    the rite you may enter but never repeat aloud,<br />
    because what is shown there<br />
    cannot be said, only undergone.</p>
    <p>I will spend years in her and never finish.<br />
    A flaw in the map?<br />
    The reason for the pilgrimage.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="section poem-movement" data-section="pharmakon-3" data-symbols="medea,poison-arrow" data-smoke-side="left" style="--section-accent: var(--accent-writings)">
  <div class="copy">
    <h3 class="mvt">III.</h3>
    <p>Her mouth is a <span lang="grc">pharmakon</span>.<br />
    One kiss carries both vials —<br />
    the venom of the changing-death,<br />
    the sting of the eighth house, the little dying<br />
    that drops the man I was<br />
    like a snake drops the winter skin<br />
    in the tall grass, and does not mourn it —<br />
    and dissolved in the same heat, the antidote:<br />
    whatever raises the newer man,<br />
    the one I could not have drafted,<br />
    the one past the border of my own imagination,<br />
    gasping, gold-veined, more.</p>
    <p>And I am the same drug in her blood.<br />
    A tongue forged in the heart of a star —<br />
    let it be venom to everything in her<br />
    that was built small by unworthy hands,<br />
    and antidote to the woman underneath,<br />
    the one who was always too vast for the room.</p>
    <p>We will trade deaths like rings.<br />
    We will resurrect each other<br />
    as a nightly discipline.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="section poem-movement" data-section="pharmakon-4" data-symbols="medea,poison-arrow" data-smoke-side="right" style="--section-accent: var(--accent-writings)">
  <div class="copy">
    <h3 class="mvt">IV.</h3>
    <p>Devotion — let me say it plainly, the way I mean it:<br />
    worship, head to sublime toe, instep, ankle.<br />
    The long ladder of the spine<br />
    climbed rung by rung with a patient mouth.<br />
    The collarbone where the pulse shows its hand.<br />
    The temple — temple, the body admits what it is —<br />
    and the crown, where I will rest my lips<br />
    like a man sealing a letter to a god.</p>
    <p>I will make a litany of her<br />
    and recite it until she believes it.<br />
    I will study her like scripture in a dying language —<br />
    every scar a gloss, every silence a canticle —<br />
    and she will read me back the same way:<br />
    not as audience, as priestess,<br />
    finding the terrible passages<br />
    and refusing to skip them,<br />
    saying this verse too, this one especially,<br />
    salvation in the sublime,<br />
    two altars facing each other,<br />
    no congregation, no witnesses,<br />
    the whole religion two members large.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="section poem-movement" data-section="pharmakon-5" data-symbols="medea,poison-arrow" data-smoke-side="left" style="--section-accent: var(--accent-writings)">
  <div class="copy">
    <h3 class="mvt">V.</h3>
    <p>But hear the secret that makes the worship possible:<br />
    only a sovereign thing can be adored.<br />
    No one kneels to an echo.<br />
    Her flame must be her own — a child, a craft,<br />
    an empire, a god I've never met —<br />
    something she tends when I am not watching,<br />
    because how she looks at what she loves<br />
    when she thinks no one sees<br />
    is the exact sight that will bring me to my knees.</p>
    <p>And she will cost me. Good.<br />
    Worship without an offering is only flattery.<br />
    Let the offering be real: my certainty, sometimes.<br />
    My schedule. My precious singular gravity.<br />
    The venom is the price of the antidote.<br />
    I will pay it in the dark, gladly,<br />
    with her name in my mouth like a coin of gold.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="section poem-movement" data-section="pharmakon-6" data-symbols="medea,poison-arrow" data-smoke-side="right" style="--section-accent: var(--accent-writings)">
  <div class="copy">
    <h3 class="mvt">VI.</h3>
    <p>So this is the refinement, the vintage of the vow:<br />
    I am done turning around.<br />
    Orpheus lost her checking for proof —<br />
    but I have learned to walk the long stair up<br />
    reading the dark itself,<br />
    feeling her there the way you feel<br />
    a second torch behind you: heat<br />
    on the nape of the neck, light<br />
    thrown past your shoulder onto the path ahead,<br />
    her breath keeping time with the song.</p>
    <p>I am building the hearth wide. Wide enough<br />
    for two infinities to sit down without folding.<br />
    And when she comes — the veiled one, the vial-bearer,<br />
    the mystery with her own armful of fire —<br />
    I will know her by a single sign:<br />
    I could not have imagined her.<br />
    That is how I'll be sure she's real.</p>
  </div>
</section>
